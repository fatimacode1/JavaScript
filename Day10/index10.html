The Solar System

Overview

The Solar System is the gravitationally bound system of the Sun and the objects that orbit it. It formed 4.6 billion years ago from the gravitational collapse of a giant interstellar molecular cloud. The vast majority (99.86%) of the system's mass is in the Sun, with most of the remaining mass contained in the planet Jupiter. The four inner system planets—Mercury, Venus, Earth and Mars—are terrestrial planets, being composed primarily of rock and metal. The four giant planets of the outer system are substantially larger and more massive than the terrestrials.

Formation & Evolution

The Solar System formed from a dense region of a molecular cloud that collapsed to form the Sun and a protoplanetary disk. The Sun grew larger by accreting mass from this disk, and the planets formed from the remaining material. Scientists estimate that the Solar System is 4.6 billion years old. The oldest known mineral grains on Earth are approximately 4.4 billion years old, suggesting that Earth formed not long after the Solar System itself. Computer simulations show that the planets likely formed through a process called accretion, where dust and gas gradually came together over millions of years. The inner planets formed closer to the Sun where it was too hot for volatile compounds to remain solid, while the outer planets formed in the cooler regions where ices could form.
